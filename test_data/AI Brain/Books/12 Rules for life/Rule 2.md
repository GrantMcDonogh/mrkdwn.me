## Core idea of Rule 2

**Treat yourself like someone you are responsible for helping.** The text argues that many people reliably care for others (even pets) better than they care for themselves—often because of **shame, self-contempt, fear, and the sense of being “fallen” or unworthy**. The rule is a corrective: behave toward yourself with the same seriousness, structure, and compassion you’d apply to someone you genuinely want to get better.

---

## Important context highlights from the text (why this is hard)

- **People often don’t take medication—even after major procedures.** The point isn’t “people are dumb,” but that self-care breaks down under depression, distrust, overwhelm, cognitive strain, cost, and isolation.
    
- **People administer meds to pets more reliably than to themselves.** The text uses this as evidence that many people implicitly believe **they don’t deserve care**, while an “innocent” being (a pet) does.
    
- **We live between order and chaos.** Order = stability, routine, tradition, structure. Chaos = uncertainty, disruption, threat, novelty, possibility. **Meaning** is found at the boundary—stable enough to function, challenged enough to grow.
    
- **Self-consciousness creates shame and avoidance.** The Genesis story is used to show why humans hide, blame, resent, and avoid responsibility once they feel exposed and judged.
    
- **Humans can knowingly do evil.** Because we understand vulnerability, we can hurt others intentionally; that knowledge fuels guilt and self-hatred, which then undermines self-care.
    
- **But there’s also a “spark of the divine.”** The counterweight to self-contempt: humans also cooperate, persist, and keep the world running through everyday heroism. You’re flawed—but not worthless.

## Specific action points (with explanations of why they matter)

### 1) **Treat your health like a duty, not a mood**

**Action:** If you’re prescribed something (meds, physio, sleep protocol, diet changes), set up a system so it gets done: reminders, weekly pill organizer, refill calendar, accountability check.  
**Why it matters:** The text’s medication examples show that “knowing” isn’t enough—**compliance fails without structure**, especially when you’re stressed, depressed, or ashamed.

### 2) **Use the “pet test” to detect self-neglect**

**Action:** Ask: _“If my dog/my best friend had my problem, what would I do for them this week?”_ Then do **that** for yourself.  
**Why it matters:** This cuts through distorted self-judgment. The text argues we often **extend care outward** more naturally than inward; borrowing that instinct bypasses shame.

### 3) **Stop confusing “good” with “what I feel like”**

**Action:** Define “good for me” like a responsible caregiver would: sleep, movement, nutrition, learning, medical follow-through, boundaries—not just comfort.  
**Why it matters:** The text explicitly says: **“good” ≠ “what you want” ≠ “what makes you happy.”** A caregiver makes a child brush teeth and wear a coat; you need the same seriousness.

### 4) **Pick a direction (a “why”) and write it down**

**Action:** Create a simple aim statement: _“I’m building a life where I’m healthy, useful, and not resentful.”_ Break it into 1–3 concrete goals for the next 3 months.  
**Why it matters:** The text frames **vision and direction as “irresistible forces”** that turn obstacles into paths. Without direction, chaos expands and you drift into bitterness.

### 5) **Put one foot in order and one foot in growth**

**Action:**

- Order: set 2–3 daily anchors (wake time, tidy 10 minutes, medication, workout slot).
    
- Growth: add one small discomfort each day (hard conversation, skill practice, applying for roles, studying).  
    **Why it matters:** Meaning lives at the border. Too much order becomes tyranny/stagnation; too much chaos becomes overwhelm. Balance is the antidote.
    

### 6) **Bargain on your own behalf (set boundaries)**

**Action:** Identify one place you’re over-giving or being exploited. Practice one clear boundary sentence and repeat it until it sticks.  
**Why it matters:** The Jung point is blunt: if you don’t stand up for yourself, **you become a slave and invite tyranny**. Self-sacrifice that enables bullying isn’t virtue—it’s self-abandonment.

### 7) **Keep promises to yourself—then reward yourself**

**Action:** Make one promise so small you can’t dodge it (e.g., “10-minute walk after lunch”). Track it daily. Reward completion weekly.  
**Why it matters:** The text says you must **trust and motivate yourself**. Trust is built by kept promises; motivation grows when effort reliably leads to reward.

### 8) **Replace global self-hatred with accurate self-respect**

**Action:** Do a two-column reality check once a week:

- “Ways I’m genuinely failing or avoiding responsibility” (specific, fixable)
    
- “Ways I’m carrying weight / showing everyday heroism” (specific evidence)  
    **Why it matters:** The text argues self-contempt is often only **half the story**. Balanced perception prevents despair while still demanding responsibility.
    

### 9) **Aim away from resentment—and name your “personal hell”**

**Action:** Write a paragraph: _“If I keep avoiding responsibility, my life in 2 years looks like…”_ Then write: _“If I treat myself like someone I’m helping, it looks like…”_  
**Why it matters:** The text repeatedly warns that lack of self-care fuels **resentment → vengeance → cruelty**. Naming the trajectory makes the cost real, and direction becomes easier.

### 10) **Act as if your wellbeing affects other people (because it does)**

**Action:** Identify who relies on you (family, partner, team, friends). Choose one self-care step that protects them indirectly (sleep, sobriety, therapy, financial order).  
**Why it matters:** The text stresses you **don’t belong only to yourself**; self-mistreatment ripples outward (it even references the devastation left by suicide). Responsibility is relational.

## Action points tailored to you (with why they matter)

### 1) Productize your self-discipline like an AI implementation

**Action:** Write a simple “operating procedure” for yourself: weekly planning, daily top 3, review loop.  
**Why it matters:** You don’t “try harder” in AI projects—you design systems that produce outcomes. Same here.

### 2) Add a “trainer” for your work focus (not motivation)

**Action:** 2–3x/week, book a 30-minute accountability call with someone (ops lead, peer entrepreneur, coach, even a disciplined friend). Agenda: “What did I ship? What’s blocked? What will ship next?”  
**Why it matters:** Your trainer works because you _show up_ when someone is waiting. This converts intention into execution.

### 3) Define “good for me” as a caregiver would

**Action:** Pick 3 non-negotiables you’d insist on for someone you care about:

- sleep cutoff / wake time
    
- deep work blocks
    
- recovery time (family + decompression)  
    **Why it matters:** Rule 2’s point: “good” ≠ “what I feel like.” Your life is demanding; without standards, chaos expands.
    

### 4) Replace goals with “delivery units”

**Action:** Every week, choose 1–2 deliverables that are _shippable_ (proposal sent, client workflow deployed, pilot measured, hire decided).  
**Why it matters:** Entrepreneurs drown in ambiguity. Shippable units create order and reduce the guilt spiral that kills self-care.

### 5) Build friction in the right direction

**Action:** Make the right actions easier and the wrong ones annoying:

- phone out of office during deep work
    
- meeting-free mornings
    
- single inbox processing time  
    **Why it matters:** You’re not weak; you’re human. Friction beats willpower.
    

### 6) Bargain on your own behalf at home and work

**Action:** Identify one recurring “yes” that breeds resentment (work scope creep, late-night Slack, too many calls). Change it to a boundary.  
**Why it matters:** The text is blunt: failing to stand up for yourself turns you into a slave and invites tyranny—then resentment follows.
